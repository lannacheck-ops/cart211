Case Notes — Data Exhaust

Data Exhaust.
That term keeps bothering me. Sounds harmless enough—like smoke from an engine, something that fades into the air and disappears. But the more I dig, the more I realize: this “exhaust” isn’t drifting anywhere. It’s sticking to me.

They say data exhaust has “no value.” If that’s true, why does it feel like they’re collecting a lot more than smoke? The GPS location of every picture I take… that’s not exhaust. That’s a breadcrumb trail of everywhere I’ve been.

Even the length of my phone calls—why keep that? What could someone learn from how long I talk, who I talk to, or how often? Feels less like leftovers and more like a dossier.

And the restaurants I eat at… the places I spend time. They call it exhaust, but these details trace patterns—habits, routines, weak spots. If someone knows where I eat, they know where to find me.

Then there’s the shopping. Not just what I buy, but why. Holiday gifts, birthday surprises, stress purchases. So data exhaust consists of things far more personal than the word “exhaust” makes it seem. It’s not fumes. It’s fingerprints—mine—left behind everywhere I go.

Whatever this system is, it’s not collecting exhaust. It’s collecting me.
